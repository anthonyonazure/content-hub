# LinkedIn Post — Red Team Your Decisions

*Byline: Anthony · VortexMSP*

---

The US Army publishes a free manual on making better decisions, and it's better than most strategy advice you'd pay for.

It's called the Red Team Handbook. About 220 pages, approved for public release. "Red teaming" means attacking your own plan on purpose — finding the holes before a competitor or a customer finds them for you. The book calls it "risk management for the human brain."

Its core claim stuck with me: most bad decisions aren't stupid. They're smart people who never questioned an assumption, agreed too fast, and confused being busy with being right.

So I did something with it. I took the book's thinking tools and turned them into prompts you can run on a business idea before you spend a dollar. A few of the sharpest:

→ Key Assumptions: "List every assumption my idea depends on, stated and unstated. For each, how badly does the idea break if it's false? Rank them and tell me which to test first."

→ Perspective Flip (this one kills the most marketing mistakes): "Role-play my target customer in their own voice. How does my offer appear to them — welcome, or noise? Flag every place I assumed they think like me."

→ Premortem: "It's six months out and this failed completely. List every reason why, uncomfortable ones included. For each, give me the cheapest test that would've caught it early."

→ Leading Indicators: "Define signals in the first 2–4 weeks that show this is actually working — not vanity metrics. Then the kill-signals that mean stop."

Then I chained all eight phases into one composite prompt and packaged it as a reusable command I run in a single word: /redteam.

Here's the difference that matters. Brainstorming tries to confirm your idea. Red teaming tries to kill it. The ideas that survive an honest premortem are the ones actually worth funding. The rest just saved you the check.

The book is free — search "UFMCS Red Team Handbook v9."
